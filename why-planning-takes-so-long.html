The Real Reason Your Planning Takes So Long

It's Sunday night, twelve tabs are open, and I still haven't started planning. The problem was never you — it's that none of your tools talk to each other.

By Ruby Smith · Co-Founder, Ivaro · K–8 Educator · July 17, 2026 · 7 min read

It's Sunday night. There are twelve tabs open. Somewhere between my lesson planner, grading rubric, state standards, parent emails, and the fourth website asking me to upload the exact same class roster.

I realize I haven't actually started planning tomorrow's lesson. I've spent the last twenty minutes getting ready to get ready. And somehow it's already 8:15.

If you've ever had a Sunday night like that, you're not alone. As teachers, we never have a shortage of tools or resources — the problem most often is that there are just too many. None of them are connected, and we never really have time to become an expert at many, or any, of them.

The Myth of More Tools = More Help

I've been teaching for just over sixteen years, and technology has always been a part of my classroom. However, the amount of platforms and options today is far greater than what was available all those years ago. With all technology, the promise is simple: technology is going to make this easier. New apps, new platforms, new tools — all of them built to help us plan better, grade faster, communicate more efficiently. But we all know that's not quite the case.

In theory, these systems have simplified many processes or provided a resource needed quickly. And honestly? Most of those tools are pretty good at what they're designed to do. That's what makes this so frustrating.

We teachers didn't get fewer things to manage. We got more. We didn't get more time. We got more tabs. I even started organizing my browser into color-coded tab groups, hoping it would make everything feel more manageable. It didn't reduce the work. It just made the chaos look more organized.

The problem isn't that you're using the wrong tools. The problem is that the tools don't work together — and nobody talks about that.

The Numbers Behind the Overload

Turns out, researchers have been measuring this too. Research from Cornerstone Communications and Edsby puts a real number to what most teachers already feel: 89% of K–12 educators rely on at least three distinct tool categories every single day. The average teacher navigates 8 to 12 different digital platforms per week. At the high school level, that number climbs to an average of 11 apps per week.

Zoom out to the district level and it gets even clearer. Most schools operate between 10 and 16 officially approved platforms — and that doesn't count the tools individual teachers find on their own, the free versions they pay for out of pocket, or the workarounds they've invented because nothing else does exactly what they need.

Some classrooms have access to up to 60 edtech tools for instruction.

Sixty! It's not hard to see why overwhelm and burnout can go hand in hand. The problem isn't a shortage of tools. It's that every new tool seems to create one more place we have to remember to check.

Researchers even have a name for this: app fatigue. The cognitive cost of constantly switching between platforms that don't share context with each other. I didn't know there was a name for it. I just knew that every tab I opened felt like one more thing my brain had to keep track of. Every time we switch platforms, our brains have to reorient. That reorientation isn't free. It costs time, it costs mental energy, and it adds up — every single day!

Reading those numbers actually made me feel better. Not because they're encouraging, but because they confirmed something I'd been feeling for years. I wasn't struggling because I couldn't keep up. I was struggling because no one was ever meant to keep up with this many disconnected systems.

For a long time, I thought I was simply exhausted from teaching. But looking back, I don't think that was true. I wasn't just tired from teaching. I was tired from managing the scaffolding around teaching. Those are two very different things.

What Disconnected Tools Actually Cost Us

Here's what app fatigue looks like in a real classroom week.

Duplicate data entry. You enter your class roster into one platform. Then another. Then another. Same students, same names, same grade level, entered manually, over and over, into platforms that were never designed to talk to each other. You're not disorganized. You're doing a job that was never designed to be done by one person.

Lost context. Your lesson plan doesn't know what your grading rubric says. Your intervention tracker doesn't know what the lesson covered last week. Your resource library doesn't know which students are struggling with which standards. Each tool holds one piece of the picture, and you are the one carrying the full picture in your head, at all times. You have become the integration layer between platforms that were built in isolation from each other.

Think about that for a moment.

Every platform assumes you'll remember what happened in the others. Somewhere along the way, we became the software integration between software that never learned to integrate itself.

The Sunday prep spiral. What should take 45 minutes takes two hours. Not because you're slow, but because every new tab is a new mental context. You close one app, open another, realize you need something from the first one, switch back, lose your place, start over. Switching apps means switching mental states. The time adds up, but so does the exhaustion.

The "good enough" trap. When you have 11 platforms to manage, you don't use any of them as well as you could. You use whatever is fastest to open, not whatever would actually serve your students best. You write the lesson plan that's quick to produce, not the one you'd really love to teach. There simply isn't time to go deeper when the overhead of managing the systems is already eating your planning time.

What a Connected Workspace Actually Changes

Imagine a different kind of Sunday. One where you're actually planning lessons, not hunting for information. One where your attention stays on your students instead of your browser tabs. One where you're thinking about how to teach instead of where you saved something.

You open one workspace. Your students are already there — with their names, their grade levels, the standards you're working toward, the data from last week's grading. You plan Monday's lesson, and the workspace already knows which students might need a different approach. You generate a practice activity that's aligned to what you just planned without opening a second tab. You check last week's grading, and it's already informing this week's differentiation, automatically.

No copy-paste. No rebuilding. No carrying the whole picture in your head because the platforms don't talk to each other.

That's what a connected workspace changes. Not just your schedule. Your experience of teaching.

Kevin and I didn't set out to build another teacher tool. We already have enough of those. We asked ourselves a different question: What if the work teachers are already doing didn't have to be spread across ten different places? That question eventually became Ivaro.

You're Not Failing at Time Management

If you're spending your Sundays managing workspaces instead of preparing to teach, that is not a personal failure.

The edtech landscape was built in pieces, by different companies, solving different problems, with no coordination between them. We were never supposed to be the glue holding it together, but somewhere along the way, that's exactly what we became. We don't need more features. We don't need another login. We don't need another webinar. We need fewer places to think. We need technology that remembers what we've already done instead of asking us to start over every time we open another tab.

Frequently Asked Questions

What apps do teachers use most for lesson planning?

Most teachers use a mix of platforms — lesson planners, grading tools, resource sites like Teachers Pay Teachers, Google Classroom, and parent communication apps — often 8–12 per week. The challenge isn't any individual tool; it's that none of them share context or connect to each other.

Why does lesson planning take so long?

Lesson planning takes so long largely because it happens in isolation from everything else. Teachers are manually syncing information — student data, standards, grading results — across multiple platforms that were never designed to work together. A connected workspace eliminates most of that duplicate work.

What is teacher app fatigue?

Teacher app fatigue is the cognitive and time cost of constantly switching between disconnected digital software. When teachers navigate 8–12 different platforms per week with no shared context between them, the mental load compounds — and over time, that compounds into burnout.
