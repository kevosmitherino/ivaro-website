Burnout Isn't About Loving Teaching Less — It's About Running on Empty Too Long

I thought I'd lost my love of teaching. It turned out I'd just been running on empty for too long — and the research shows I was far from alone.

By Ruby Smith · Co-Founder, Ivaro · K–8 Educator · July 15, 2026 · 7 min read

Pulling into my driveway an hour to an hour and a half after school got out, and I only live ten minutes from the school. It's quiet, it's peaceful, but when I go inside, now my family needs me. I take a few deep breaths and then go inside. I try to focus on my husband, my kids, and just be present with my family. But my mind just keeps getting pulled to the list in my head of everything that didn't get done and what I'll need to do tomorrow before the students arrive.

Anyone else been there?

Teaching Was Never Meant to Be a 24-Hour Job

The last couple of years in the classroom, I started to feel as though I had lost my love of teaching. I went to work an hour and a half early and stayed after contract time at least an hour every day, but never felt caught up, still couldn't keep up with all the demands, and sometimes just found it difficult to even just go to work! Then, during breaks or even the summer, I found myself missing being in the classroom, getting a head start on lesson plans or things I want to try with my students, and then, it hit me. I didn't "lose" my love of teaching — there was just too much on my plate. I was burnt out.

Burnout Isn't a Passion Problem

Sometimes I felt guilty for not being as passionate about teaching or my students as I used to be, but then I had to learn what burnout actually meant. Burnout isn't about losing my passion. It's not me being lazy on those days when I just don't "feel" like doing anything. It's not being a bad teacher, and it doesn't mean I made a bad career choice.

Burnout is actually quite the opposite, and it's much more common than you may realize. Burnout is what happens when deeply caring people are asked to carry an impossible workload for too long.

The World Health Organization (WHO) classifies burnout as an occupational phenomenon resulting from chronic, unmanaged workplace stress. It is clinically defined by three distinct dimensions:

  • Energy Depletion or Exhaustion: A pervasive feeling of being emotionally and physically drained.
  • Mental Distance or Cynicism: Increased negativism, detachment, or feelings of cynicism related to one's job.
  • Reduced Professional Efficacy: A distinct sense of ineffectiveness, lack of accomplishment, and diminished productivity.
The teachers who burn out aren't the ones who cared too little. They're the ones who cared too much for too long without enough support.

The Numbers Tell the Story

What does the research on burnout tell us?

According to the RAND Corporation's 2025 State of the American Teacher survey, 53% of K–12 teachers reported feeling burned out — and that's actually an improvement from prior years. But the numbers beneath that headline tell the real story of why.

Teachers' contracts are typically written for 21–40 hours per week. Yet 88% of teachers actually work 41 to more than 80 hours per week. The gap isn't filled by choice — it's filled by all the work that can't fit into the school day: lesson planning at the kitchen table, grading on Sunday morning, answering parent emails after the kids are in bed.

Research from Education Week found that teachers average 53 hours per week — seven more than comparable working adults — and that one in four of those hours goes completely uncompensated.

The personal cost is just as striking. RAND's research found that 46% of teachers say they cannot enjoy their personal life because of work demands. For other working adults in similar roles? Just 13%.

Based on the research, you can see — you're not alone. Just like I wasn't alone when I realized what was going on, that I was suffering from burnout. While it's nice to know others feel the same, it doesn't present a solution or alleviate the problem. And the problem isn't motivation. It's the math. Teachers literally do not have enough time to meet all the demands.

Where All the Time Goes

You know the demands — no one has to tell you how long the to-do list is. But what so many people fail to understand is the why. There's too much to do and not enough time because the majority of your day you're teaching students. Every teacher knows that "prep" time isn't nearly enough time to lesson plan, grade, plan interventions, make parent phone calls, answer emails, and even get a chance to use the restroom. Then there are meetings, and then more meetings, district trainings, and other requirements that take more time away. Many of us show up early and leave late, and somehow the list never gets shorter. While none of the tasks are impossible, it just becomes overwhelming because there simply isn't enough time.

When You Know You're Running on Empty

Have you ever...

  • dreaded Sunday afternoons?
  • felt guilty relaxing?
  • snapped at your family over something small?
  • laid in bed thinking about tomorrow's lesson?
  • wondered if you're the only teacher struggling?

If you answered yes to several of those, you're probably not failing.

You're probably exhausted.

Frequently Asked Questions

What causes teacher burnout?

Teacher burnout is most commonly caused by chronic overwork, insufficient planning time, high administrative load, disconnected tools, and lack of instructional support — not a lack of passion for teaching.

How many hours a week do teachers actually work?

Most teachers work 41–80+ hours per week, despite contracts written for 21–40 hours. Research from Education Week found the average is 53 hours per week, with one in four hours going completely uncompensated.

How do you recover from teacher burnout?

Recovery requires more than rest — it requires reducing the actual workload. That means better planning systems, connected tools that eliminate duplicate work, and access to instructional support on demand, not just once a year in a professional development session.
